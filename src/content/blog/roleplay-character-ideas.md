The hardest part of starting an AI roleplay isn't writing the character — it's deciding *who* to play in the first place. You open a blank character field, the cursor blinks, and every idea feels either too generic ("a mysterious stranger") or too complicated to pin down. The blank page wins more often than anyone admits.

The fastest way through it is to stop starting from nothing and start from an **archetype** — a proven behavioral spine that already knows how it acts. An archetype isn't a cliché; it's a foundation. It gives the AI a clear, consistent core to hold onto (which is half the battle against a character drifting out of personality), and it gives *you* a frame to make specific. Below are eight archetypes that hold up in AI roleplay, what each is good for, and how to turn it into a playable character in minutes. If you want to skip straight to a finished draft, the free [AI Character Card Generator](/tools/character-card-generator) rolls a complete character built on these exact archetypes — but it's worth understanding what you're working with first.

## Why Start From an Archetype?

Because the single most important thing to settle about a character isn't their hair colour or their backstory — it's **how they relate to you, the player.** Mentor, rival, protector, threat: that relationship shapes every line they'll ever say. An archetype defines it up front, which is exactly the kind of clear, concrete anchor that keeps an AI in character instead of melting into a generic helpful voice. From there, you make it specific. A character described only as "kind and mysterious" gives the model nothing to act on; "a guarded mentor who never says thank you and repays kindness with action" gives it everything. (That specificity principle is the whole of our guide on [how to write a character card](/blog/how-to-write-a-character-card).)

## The 8 Archetypes

### 1. The Mentor

A demanding guide who sees more in you than you see in yourself — and treats that potential as a reason to be hard on you rather than soft. The Mentor works because it builds an immediate dynamic with real stakes: approval has to be earned, and that makes earning it satisfying. **Make it yours:** give them one thing they refuse to just *tell* you (the lesson is in finding it), and one small tell that they actually care. Great for long campaigns where you want to visibly grow.

### 2. The Rival

Someone who measures themselves against you constantly and respects nothing that wasn't earned the hard way. The Rival generates friction, and friction is what most "nice" AI characters lack. The needling, the competitiveness, the grudging respect underneath — it keeps scenes charged. **Make it yours:** decide what you're actually competing *over*, and let the rivalry hide something closer to respect than either of you will admit.

### 3. The Rogue

All charm on the surface, all calculation underneath. The Rogue plays every angle and keeps an exit in sight, which makes them magnetic and slightly dangerous to trust. They're a favourite for a reason: the tension between the easy smile and the hidden agenda drives endless good scenes. **Make it yours:** give them a loyalty that's real but conditional — and make the conditions theirs, not yours.

### 4. The Guardian

Protective, duty-bound, slow to trust and immovable once they do. Where the Rogue keeps you guessing, the Guardian gives you a rock. They work beautifully as companions because the slow earning of their trust is a story in itself. **Make it yours:** decide *why* they've appointed themselves your protector — guilt, a debt, a promise to someone offstage — and never let them make a speech about it.

### 5. The Wildcard

Unpredictable, follows a private logic, and delights in being three steps somewhere nobody expected — while being far more perceptive than the chaos suggests. The Wildcard keeps a session from ever getting comfortable. **Make it yours:** the trick is to make the chaos *legible in hindsight* — their choices should make perfect sense once you understand the logic, which keeps them a character rather than a random-number generator.

### 6. The Confidant

Reads people the way other people read weather, and uses it to draw them out rather than to corner them. The Confidant is the archetype for emotionally-driven, slow-burn roleplay: warm, perceptive, impossible to lie to, and a vault for whatever you hand over. **Make it yours:** give them a reason they're so good at reading people (and so reluctant to be read themselves). Pairs naturally with relationship-focused campaigns.

### 7. The Antagonist

Genuinely convinced they're the reasonable one — which makes their courtesy more unsettling than any threat. A good antagonist doesn't twirl a moustache; they offer you every chance to agree, right up until the moment that chance closes. **Make it yours:** write their logic so it *almost* convinces you. The best villains are the ones where you can see exactly how they got there. Use a clear hard limit so the menace stays in control rather than tipping into cartoon.

### 8. The Lost Soul

Carries something from before that they won't set down, and has gotten very good at being almost-present. The Lost Soul is built for stories about reconnection: guarded, haunted, and quietly hoping you'll stay long enough to matter. **Make it yours:** decide what they survived without walking away from — and let them keep you at arm's length while clearly wanting the opposite.

## Turning an Archetype Into a Playable Character

An archetype is the spine; a character is the spine plus specifics. Three additions turn any of the eight above into something the AI can actually play well:

1. **One hard limit** — something the character will *not* do, stated plainly. Models follow concrete prohibitions far better than they infer them, and a limit is what gives a persona a backbone.
2. **One distinct way of speaking** — clipped and terse, formal to a fault, dry and deadpan. Voice is what a reader feels first.
3. **One motivation they won't discuss directly** — the thing driving them under the surface, which gives every scene a current.

Write those onto the archetype and you have a character card.
