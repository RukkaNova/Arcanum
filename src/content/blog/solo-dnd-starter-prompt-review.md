Not every entry here needs three sections to explain. [Solo D&D Starter](/games/solo-dnd-starter-prompt), published by Matthew Maynor as part of a copy-paste prompt guide, is a single sentence: "Act as a Dungeon Master. Create a simple D&D-style solo adventure for a level [X] character. Include a setting, objective, and first encounter." That's the entire prompt, and this review is short because the game is too.

## What You Get For One Line

Drop it into Claude with a level filled in and you get exactly what it asks for — a setting, a stated objective, and a first encounter, narrated from there as a freeform conversation. No stat block, no dice, no rules layer. Its whole value is speed: zero setup between deciding you want a solo session and being in one.

## What You Don't Get

Everything past that first scene is you and the model improvising with nothing to lean on. There's no persistence if you close the chat, no world that exists independently of what you're told in the moment, and no mechanical resistance if you decide your character can do anything at all. It's a conversation starter, not a system.

## Verdict

Judge Solo D&D Starter for what it is — a fast on-ramp into a solo session, useful the way a good icebreaker question is useful — and it does its one job. Judge it as a game with any staying power and it has none. For something with actual mechanical weight behind it, see the [best Claude RPG games](/blog/best-claude-rpgs) or a fully-engineered system like [Aevum Realm Architect](/blog/aevum-realm-architect).

**Solo D&D Starter Prompt Rating: 2/5** — Gets a session started in one line and does nothing else.
