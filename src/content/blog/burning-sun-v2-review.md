[Burning Sun V2](/games/burning-sun-v2) is not a packaged game you launch — it's a prompt you paste into Gemini, shared publicly on Reddit by a user named No_Calligrapher_4557. That distinction matters less than you'd think, because what's interesting about it isn't the delivery, it's the design philosophy baked into the instructions. This is a prompt with an actual thesis about how an RPG should behave, and one line of it tells you everything: *"Do not let the character happen to the world, but rather, have the world happen to the character."*

Most AI RPGs are wish-fulfilment engines that bend reality toward the player. Burning Sun V2 explicitly tries to invert that, and the result is a game with genuinely novel ideas and an equally clear ceiling. This review is about both — what the reactivity buys, where it stalls, and who should paste it in.

## What Burning Sun V2 Is

You paste the prompt and Gemini becomes "The Guide," a deliberately hands-off DM that sets things up and then refuses to talk to you directly again. Character creation collects your traits, abilities, backstory, and appearance, then you pick a setting — Classic Medieval, Victorian, Cyberpunk, a Star Wars-style Galaxy, or Current Day, with a build-your-own option — and choose a storyteller temperament. Once you're in, every message opens with a compact stat header tracking day, time, current activity, currency, and a mood emoji, and the simulation runs from there.

It's lightweight by design. There's no companion app, no save system beyond your chat history, and the author is upfront that it was written to instruct model behaviour rather than to read as polished prose. As a Gemini prompt it slots neatly alongside the other options in the [best Gemini RPG games](/blog/best-gemini-rpg-games) roundup, with a different bet than most of them.

## The Reactivity Is the Hook

The standout feature is what I'd call proto-reactivity. NPCs react to your deeds, and the consequences of your actions ripple outward in small, believable ways — the prompt's own example is buying an apple: your currency drops, the merchant is down one apple, and if it was his last sale before closing, that lands too. Commit a crime in front of a crowd and the world responds in proportion. It's not a full simulation, but it's a real attempt at one, and it gives play a texture most prompt-RPGs lack.

The other clever idea is the storyteller modes. You pick a temperament — wrathful, regular, or peculiar — and it colours the entire tone of the story. Wrathful leans aggressive, pressured, and bleak; the others bend the other way. Which one you prefer is subjective, but wrathful was easily the most interesting to me, because it gives the world a point of view instead of a neutral narrator politely accommodating you. That single choice does more to differentiate a playthrough than most character sheets do.

## But the NPCs Won't Lead

Here's the ceiling. The reactivity is real, but it's only ever *re*-activity. The NPCs respond well to what you do and then wait for the next thing you do. They lack initiative — they rarely start trouble, pursue their own goals off-screen, or walk into your story uninvited. The prompt asks for characters who "are living as actual persons" doing "their own things," but in practice the world mostly idles until you poke it, then reacts convincingly to the poke.

This is the gap between the prompt's ambition and what a stateless model can actually sustain. Genuine NPC initiative requires the system to track and advance background agendas turn after turn, and a prompt has no memory to hold those agendas in — as the conversation grows, anything not directly in front of the model fades. (I dug into why this happens in [why LLMs forget](/blog/why-llms-forget).) It's the same wall every prompt-driven RPG hits; Burning Sun V2 just gets closer to it than most before stopping, which is both a compliment and the core of its 3/5.

## The World-Agnostic Trade

The second limitation is one Burning Sun V2 shares with [Solo RPG Master](/blog/solo-rpg-master-review): it's a generalist with no world of its own. There's no lore in the prompt, so the settings are only as deep as the cliché you request — a competent medieval kingdom, a serviceable cyberpunk sprawl, the version you already pictured. The "Galaxy" option goes further and literally instructs the model to copy Star Wars lore wholesale, which is fun as a sandbox but the opposite of original.

It's worth seeing this as a deliberate spectrum rather than a flaw. At one end sits a world-first game like [Isekai RPG](/blog/isekai-rpg-review), which hands you a richly designed setting but drifts away from its own rules. At the other end sit world-agnostic engines like Solo RPG Master and Burning Sun V2, which stay coherent precisely because they commit to no canon — and therefore can't surprise you with any. Burning Sun's wrinkle is that it spends its design energy on *reactivity* rather than world or character depth, which is a genuinely different and welcome bet, even if it doesn't escape the generalist trap.

## Who It's For

Paste in Burning Sun V2 if you're curious about a more consequence-driven kind of AI roleplay and you like the idea of a world that pushes back — especially in wrathful mode, and especially if you bring your own setting ideas rather than expecting the prompt to supply them. It's free, it's a single copy-paste, and it does something most prompts don't even attempt.

Look elsewhere if you want deep, original worldbuilding out of the box, or NPCs who drive the plot without you. For the wider field, the [public games directory](/games) is the place to compare it against world-first and character-first alternatives.

## Verdict

Burning Sun V2 is a simple prompt with a real idea. Its proto-reactivity and storyteller modes are novel enough to make it worth a session, and the "world happens to the character" philosophy is a refreshing inversion of the usual power fantasy. It's held back by NPCs that react but won't lead, and by the world-agnostic design that trades originality for consistency. A good, capable game that does exactly what it sets out to do — no more, but no less.

**Burning Sun V2 Rating: 3/5** — A novel, consequence-driven Gemini prompt with clever storyteller modes, capped by reactive-but-passive NPCs and generalist, lore-free worlds.
