Before you spend an hour browsing Janitor AI's catalogue, there is one question worth asking yourself: do you want to talk to a character, or do you want to play a game?

If the answer is a character — a specific persona, a fictional person, an archetype you want to explore — Janitor AI is probably the most useful tool that exists for what you want. If the answer is a game — a world with stakes, consequences that stack, choices that matter beyond the current conversation — then Janitor AI will feel hollow within a few sessions, no matter how much time you spend finding the right card.

That distinction is the whole review, really. But the practical questions — how to actually find something worth using, when to connect your own model, how to read card quality before committing — those deserve more than a one-liner.

## What You're Actually Walking Into

Janitor AI is a character-sharing platform. Users create character cards — documents specifying a persona's personality, voice, backstory, scenario context, and example dialogue — and publish them for anyone to use. Other users find them, launch a session, and have a conversation with that character. That's the loop.

The catalogue is genuinely enormous. We're talking hundreds of thousands of characters across every genre, every franchise, every archetype, and every content rating. There is almost certainly a card for whatever you're looking for. The platform's critical mass of user content is its most defensible advantage — no curated platform can match it on sheer variety.

What you are not walking into is a game engine. There is no world state, no persistent memory beyond what the current context window holds, no mechanics, no consequence system. Janitor AI is a place to have a conversation with a character. If that sounds limiting, it is — but it's also a specific, real thing that a large number of people want.

## The Library Experience: What Finding a Good Card Actually Looks Like

This is where new users either find their footing or give up.

The search and discovery experience is functional rather than curated. Cards surface by popularity, recency, and tags, and popular does not reliably mean good — it often means early. A card that accumulated downloads in 2024 stays visible regardless of whether the underlying writing was any good by current standards.

When evaluating an unfamiliar card, the things that actually predict session quality are not the rating or download count. They are: the length and specificity of the personality field (vague cards produce vague characters), the quality of the example dialogue (if the sample messages are generic, the sessions will be too), and whether the scenario description gives the model something concrete to work from or just an atmosphere to gesture at.

Cards written with loose, impressionistic personality descriptions tend to produce characters who shift to match whatever tone the conversation takes rather than holding their own. That can feel like good responsiveness at first and like a lack of identity by hour two. Cards with tight, specific instructions for how the character speaks, what they care about, what they will and won't do — those hold up.

The good news: once you can read a card before launching it, the library becomes genuinely useful. The bad news: it takes a few bad sessions to develop that instinct. (The same instinct works in reverse when you build your own — our guide on [how to write a character card](/blog/how-to-write-a-character-card) breaks down what actually makes one hold up.)

## The BYOM Decision

Janitor AI runs its own model — Janitor LLM — for users who don't want to deal with API keys. For casual users who want to dip into the library without setup friction, this is fine. For anyone who has spent time with frontier models, the quality gap will be apparent quickly: shorter responses, weaker adherence to character instructions in longer exchanges, and less nuanced prose.

The bring-your-own-model feature lets you route sessions through your own Claude, OpenAI, or compatible API key. This changes the experience substantially. The same character card that feels flat on Janitor LLM can feel genuinely alive when a frontier model like Claude or GPT is doing the generation. Character instructions hold longer, responses develop more internal texture, and the conversation can sustain complexity that the in-house model starts simplifying around turn fifteen.

The cost question is the practical one. If you already have a Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus subscription, connecting your API key adds only the marginal token cost of sessions — typically a few cents for a normal conversation, more if you're running long exchanges on Opus-tier models. That math is usually favourable compared to paying a second subscription elsewhere. If you don't already have an API key, you're making a different decision: whether to set one up specifically for Janitor AI, which is likely worth it only if the library itself is what draws you.

Setup takes about ten minutes. ## What Kind of Roleplay Actually Works Here

Character-driven one-on-one conversation is where Janitor AI is at its best. The format rewards a player who wants to inhabit a relationship with a specific persona — a companion, a rival, a mentor, a character from a piece of fiction they care about. The lack of game mechanics is not a weakness in this context; it is an absence of friction.

Social and ensemble roleplay — conversations involving multiple characters interacting — works up to a point. The Groups feature exists and is functional. But longer multi-character threads tend to see voices blur as the context fills: characters start responding with similar rhythms, distinct speaking patterns flatten, and the model's job of tracking multiple personas simultaneously visibly strains. Solo sessions are more reliable.

What doesn't work: anything that requires the platform to remember something from a previous session, track a resource, enforce a rule consistently across dozens of exchanges, or give you the feeling that your choices have weight beyond the immediate conversation. Janitor AI has no mechanism for any of that. Players who have been using engineered RPG systems — games built around persistent state and mechanical structure — will find the lack of scaffolding jarring. The session ends, and nothing carries forward.

## The Content Question

Janitor AI is explicitly more permissive than the native interfaces of the models it connects to. The character card layer mediates prompts in ways that can expand what a model will engage with, and the platform's own content policy is lighter than Claude's or OpenAI's direct product surfaces.

For players who left Claude or ChatGPT because dramatic scenes — conflict, moral complexity, dark themes — were being sanitised mid-session, this matters. The difference is real enough that it is a primary reason some users choose the platform over running their preferred model directly. For the full set of no-filter options — permissive platforms, bring-your-own-model, and local models — see our [uncensored AI roleplay guide](/blog/uncensored-ai-roleplay).

The explicit content question is more nuanced. The platform has NSFW-flagged content and age verification requirements for it. Whether the platform's handling of this is adequate is a separate debate; what's true is that the presence of this content affects the catalogue's overall character and is worth knowing going in if you're not looking for it.

But the comparison that comes up most often once you're here: why use Janitor AI instead of just opening a Claude Project or a Custom GPT and writing your own character setup?

The honest answer is that most players won't write as good a card as the best creators in the Janitor AI library. The platform has authors who have spent hundreds of hours refining specific characters — testing how instructions interact with model behaviour, iterating on dialogue examples, stress-testing edge cases. For niche characters or specific archetypes, the best available card on Janitor AI is probably better than what a typical user would write from scratch in twenty minutes.

The counterargument: if you know what you want and can articulate it, a Claude Project gives you persistent memory across sessions, tighter instruction adherence, and the ability to attach knowledge files — things Janitor AI cannot replicate. The right choice depends on whether the library is solving your problem or whether you have a specific vision that needs a blank canvas.

## The Verdict

Janitor AI is genuinely useful, specifically scoped, and widely misunderstood. People arrive expecting a game and find a conversation platform; they arrive expecting consistent quality and find a library that requires curation. Neither of those things makes it bad — they make it a tool with a specific job.

For the player who wants to talk to characters rather than run campaigns, who already has a frontier model subscription they're willing to route through it, and who can spend a session or two developing an eye for card quality — this is the best character library that exists, and the model flexibility makes the economics work in a way that no fixed-platform subscription does.

For the player who wants a world to remember them, a ruleset to push against, or stakes that persist beyond the current context window — this is the wrong tool, and no amount of good cards will change that.

**Janitor AI Rating: 4/5** — The largest AI character library available, made meaningfully more useful by the BYOM feature. Scope it correctly and it's excellent; scope it wrong and it will disappoint.
