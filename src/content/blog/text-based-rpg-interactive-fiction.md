**AI interactive fiction** is the umbrella term: any story a language model generates live, in response to what you type, rather than one an author pre-wrote and branched into pages. **Text-based RPG** is the narrower, play-focused half of it — you control one character, the AI runs everyone and everything else, and the story is something that happens *to* you rather than something you write. The two phrases get used as synonyms constantly, and for casual searching that's fine. But they point at genuinely different relationships to the text, and picking the wrong one is why some people bounce off a tool that would have suited them perfectly under the other name.

This page draws that line clearly, then routes you to what actually fits each side — without re-ranking platforms we've already reviewed in depth elsewhere.

## Where Both Terms Come From

Text adventures are old — *Zork*, *Colossal Cave*, the *Choose Your Own Adventure* books — all built on a fixed script an author wrote in advance. Swap that script for a live language model and you get what we call an [AI text adventure](/blog/what-is-an-ai-text-adventure): every scene generated for exactly what you do next, with no page 47 waiting to be turned to. "Text-based RPG" and "AI interactive fiction" are both descendants of that same shift, and the full history and mechanics of it live on that page. What's worth adding here is the split those older terms don't capture on their own.

## The Real Split: Playing a Character vs. Writing One

Every AI storytelling tool sits somewhere on one axis: **how much of the text is yours to shape directly.**

At the play end, you're a character *inside* the story. You type what you do — *I draw my sword* — and the AI decides what happens next: whether the guard reacts, whether the blade lands, what it costs you. You don't write the guard's dialogue or narrate your own success. Agency rules exist specifically to enforce this — the AI describes the world and waits, and it never puts words in your character's mouth. This is the text-based RPG experience, and it's a game in the fullest sense: choices, consequences, a world that pushes back.

At the write end, you're the *author*. You're not confined to one character's actions — you can write any part of the scene, including prose you didn't type yourself but want to keep. You retry a passage that didn't land, edit a line the AI wrote, branch the plot in a direction you decide rather than one your character's actions earned. The AI is a collaborator generating raw material for you to shape, not a game master adjudicating what you're allowed to do. This is AI interactive fiction in its purest form — closer to co-writing a novel than playing a character in one.

Neither is "more real" AI storytelling. They're different jobs. Our own [NovelAI review](/blog/novelai-review) opens on exactly this question — *do you want to write a story, or do you want to play one* — because getting the answer wrong is the single most common reason someone tries a tool built for the other mode and comes away disappointed.

## Text-Based RPG: What "Playing" Actually Means

If you want to be a character and let the world react to you, you're looking for a text-based RPG. The defining features:

- **Agency rules.** The AI narrates the world and NPCs; it never narrates your character's thoughts, words, or actions for you.
- **Consequence, not editing.** If your plan fails, it fails — you don't get to rewrite the outcome after the fact the way an author would revise a page.
- **A game master, not a co-author.** Someone (or something) is running the game with rules, even loose ones, rather than collaborating on prose with you as an equal.

This is the territory covered end-to-end in [what an AI text adventure actually is](/blog/what-is-an-ai-text-adventure) and [how an AI Dungeon Master works](/blog/ai-dungeon-master) — both walk through the full spectrum from a freeform chat session to platforms like [AI Dungeon](/clients/ai-dungeon) and [RoleForge](/clients/roleforge) to fully engineered systems like the [Arcanum Originals](/arcanum-games). If playing is what you're after, start there rather than here — this page's job is just pointing you in the right direction.

## AI Interactive Fiction: What "Writing" Actually Means

If you want to shape a story directly rather than live inside one character's choices, you want a tool built for authors, not players. The defining features:

- **Editorial control.** Retry a passage, rewrite a line, undo a turn you don't like — the text is yours to revise, not a record of what "actually happened."
- **Worldbuilding tools over game rules.** Instead of agency rules and consequence tracking, you get lorebooks, entry banks, and reference systems that keep a sprawling setting consistent as you write it.
- **You, not a character sheet.** There's no fixed "your character" the system is protecting your agency over — you can write any voice, including the narrator's.

[NovelAI](/blog/novelai-review) is the clearest example on our roster: a fiction-tuned writing studio built around an editor, not a chat window, with a Lorebook designed for authors managing a long manuscript. [SillyTavern](/blog/sillytavern-guide) does the same worldbuilding job through World Info — free, on whatever model you connect, with more raw editing and memory tooling layered on top — and we've compared the two head-to-head in [SillyTavern vs NovelAI](/blog/sillytavern-vs-novelai), framed on this exact write-a-story-vs-play-one axis. [Dunia](/blog/dunia-review) sits closer to the middle of the whole spectrum this page describes: a generated, narrator-run world like a text-based RPG, but with an author's toolkit — edit any message, branch into an alternate timeline — that leans firmly toward the writing side. Worth trying if NovelAI's dedicated-editor structure feels heavier than you want.

## Where the Line Blurs

The split above is real, but it's a spectrum, not two sealed rooms. A few places it blurs on purpose:

**SillyTavern does both.** Nothing stops you from running strict agency rules through a system prompt and playing it as a text-based RPG, or dropping the rules and treating it as a pure writing tool. The interface is the same either way — the mode lives in your prompt, not the software.

**Character cards bridge the two.** A well-written [character card](/blog/how-to-write-a-character-card) defines a persona precisely enough to hold up whether you're playing opposite them in a game or co-writing scenes with them as a collaborator.

**Engineered RPGs still feel authored.** The [Arcanum Originals](/arcanum-games) are built as text-based RPGs — agency rules, consequence, a real game master — but a deep world bible and a companion with real emotional continuity (see [The Chronicler](/blog/the-chronicler)) can feel as narratively rich as anything on the writing side. Structure and good prose aren't opposites.

If you like both modes, that's not a contradiction — it's most people. Play a campaign when you want to be surprised; open a writing tool when you know exactly the scene you want and just need help getting there.

## Which One Do You Actually Want?

A fast filter, if the split above didn't already answer it for you:

**You want to be surprised by what happens** → text-based RPG. Start with [AI Dungeon](/clients/ai-dungeon) for zero setup or the [Arcanum Originals](/arcanum-games) for a deeper engineered system.

**You already know the scene and want help writing it well** → AI interactive fiction. Start with [NovelAI](/blog/novelai-review) for a polished all-in-one studio, or [SillyTavern](/blog/sillytavern-guide) if you want free, model-agnostic control.

**You want a persistent world you can return to either way** → SillyTavern's Lorebook/World Info, or a [Claude Project / Gemini Gem / Custom GPT](/blog/rpg-prompts) you configure yourself to lean whichever direction you like.

**You're not sure and just want to try something right now** → [AI RPG chat](/blog/ai-rpg-chat) covers running a freeform session in a plain chat window with zero setup — the fastest way to feel out which mode you actually prefer before committing to a platform.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is AI interactive fiction?**

It splits into two experiences depending on how much authorial control you want: playing a character inside a world someone else (the AI) narrates, or writing and steering a story as its co-author.

**What is a text-based RPG?**
