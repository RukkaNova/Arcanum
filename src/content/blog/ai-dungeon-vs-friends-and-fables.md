**AI Dungeon vs Friends & Fables** isn't really a fight between two similar products — it's a choice between two different ideas of what an AI RPG should be. AI Dungeon is a *sandbox*: type anything, and the AI improvises a story with you, no rules in the way. Friends & Fables is a *game*: stats, dice, a tactical map, and an AI Game Master that runs an actual ruleset and remembers what happened. One maximises freedom. The other maximises structure. Almost everything that separates them follows from that.

This is a head-to-head on what actually decides it — structure, memory, multiplayer, creative freedom, ease of starting, and price — so you can tell which idea of an AI RPG is the one you want.

## What Each One Actually Is

AI Dungeon, built by Latitude, is the platform that defined the freeform AI text adventure. Its whole identity is the absence of constraint — no character sheets, no dice, no hard-coded rules. You type what you want to do and the AI continues the story, whether that's a dragon-riding space pirate or a kingdom run by sentient vegetables. That improvisational openness is its enduring appeal, covered in full in our [AI Dungeon review](/blog/ai-dungeon-review) (rated 3.5/5).

Friends & Fables comes at the same medium from the opposite pole. It's a structured AI tabletop — closer to a virtual tabletop with an AI Dungeon Master than to a chat window. Its AI Game Master, **Franz**, isn't a single chatbot; it's an orchestration layer that routes each turn to up to a dozen specialized models — one for lore, one for the character sheet, one for map coordinates, one for narrative — then assembles the result. That architecture is why it can run a real ruleset instead of just describing one. The full breakdown is in our [Friends & Fables review](/blog/friends-and-fables-review) (rated 4.5/5).

If you're new to the whole category, our [AI Dungeon Master guide](/blog/ai-dungeon-master) explains how AI DMs work and where each style fits.

## Structure and Mechanics

This is the fault line. AI Dungeon has essentially no mechanics by design — there's nothing to learn and nothing to push against, which is exactly what some players want and exactly what leaves others feeling like their choices don't carry weight. Succeeding at something feels the same as the AI simply agreeing with you.

Friends & Fables runs a genuine game underneath the fiction: stats, ability checks, initiative, and a V3 hex-battlemap combat system that handles line-of-sight, cover, resistances, and vulnerabilities. A charisma check succeeding feels different from the AI going along with you, because there's a system adjudicating it. For players who found AI Dungeon directionless, that structure is the entire point. For players who found rules a chore, it's overhead. Neither is wrong — they're opposite preferences.

## Memory and Consistency

Here Friends & Fables wins decisively, and it's the difference that matters most over a long campaign.

AI Dungeon's memory is fragile. Because it treats every session as one continuous text-generation task, it hallucinates past events, occasionally resurrects characters who died, and loses spatial and plot logic as the context window fills. Premium tiers expand the window, but the architecture still struggles to hold a campaign together over dozens of sessions.

Friends & Fables attacks the problem at the design level with its **Working Context** — a transparent, player-editable record the platform builds before each response, deliberately filtering for only the lore, character states, and environment relevant to the current scene. The effect is that the NPC you built a relationship with three sessions ago still remembers it, and the sword you lost to a river doesn't quietly reappear. It's the most consistent long-term memory of any platform in this comparison. The underlying reason memory is hard for *every* tool is worth understanding in [why AI campaigns fall apart](/blog/why-llms-forget) — Friends & Fables doesn't repeal that limit, it manages it better than almost anyone.

## Multiplayer

If you want to play with other people, this isn't close. Friends & Fables supports real synchronous multiplayer for up to six players, with individual character tracking, split-party management, and shared tactical maps — a genuine substitute for a tabletop session when the group can't meet in person.

AI Dungeon's multiplayer exists but is casual and asynchronous by nature — fine for collaborative storytelling passed between friends, not a replacement for everyone playing a game together in real time. For group play, Friends & Fables is built for it; AI Dungeon merely allows it.

## Creative Freedom

The one axis where AI Dungeon takes a clear point back. Nothing matches its ceiling for sheer "anything goes" improvisation — surreal, genre-bending, dream-logic stories that no ruleset would tolerate. If being the author of something genuinely strange is the goal, AI Dungeon is the better canvas.

Friends & Fables is expressive — the community has built X-Men simulations, grimdark MorkBorg adaptations, and crime-syndicate dramas, so it's far from locked to high fantasy — but you're always working within a system. That system is what gives outcomes weight, and it's also what bounds the chaos. Freedom vs. structure, again.

## Ease of Starting and Price

AI Dungeon wins the on-ramp and the free end. You're playing within seconds, and after the **Unchained update** its free tier is genuinely usable, ad-free, without the old energy systems. For trying AI storytelling tonight for nothing, it's the obvious door. Its catch is at the *top*: the highest Mythic/Ultimate tiers can run $50–100/month, which is hard to justify for a text experience. (More on the free tier in [is AI Dungeon free?](/blog/is-ai-dungeon-free).)

Friends & Fables is a premium product with only a limited trial — meaningful play means subscribing, roughly $19.95/month (Starter, four players) up to $39.95/month (Legend, six players), with two months free on annual billing. It also asks more of you: it's not a system that runs cleanly on autopilot, and it can occasionally force combat when a social scene would have been better. For a group replacing a standing tabletop night, the math works. For a casual solo dabble, it's a lot.

## Which Should You Choose?

A quick filter:

**Choose AI Dungeon if** you want free, instant, unrestricted solo storytelling, you value improvisation over rules, and you don't mind narrative inconsistency. It's the better sandbox and the better free option.

**Choose Friends & Fables if** you want a real game — mechanics, tactical combat, and outcomes that carry weight — and especially if you're playing with a group. It's the closest thing to a competent human DM, and you'll get the most from it if you engage with its depth and pay for it. **[Full review](/blog/friends-and-fables-review)** (rated 4.5/5).
