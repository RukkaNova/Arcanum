**Voyage vs AI Dungeon** is an unusual head-to-head: both platforms come from the same studio, Latitude, and Voyage exists specifically because Latitude noticed what players kept complaining about in AI Dungeon and built the opposite answer. AI Dungeon runs on pure narrative fiat — nothing you do can really fail, because the story accommodates you no matter what. Voyage introduces real dice, hit points, and permanent character death, so that failure and risk actually mean something.

Same lineage of technology, rebuilt around an opposite premise. This breaks down what actually separates them — mechanics, memory, multiplayer, platform, and price — so you can tell which philosophy fits how you want to play.

## What Each One Actually Is

[AI Dungeon](/clients/ai-dungeon) is the platform that defined the freeform AI text adventure back in 2019. No character sheets, no dice, no hard-coded rules — you type what you want to do and the AI continues the story, whatever direction that takes. Our [full AI Dungeon review](/blog/ai-dungeon-review) rates it 3.5/5.

[Voyage](/clients/voyage) is Latitude's direct answer to the frustration that builds up over long AI Dungeon sessions: the realization that nothing you do actually matters, because the AI is infinitely persuadable and the story bends around you regardless of how badly you fail. Voyage's proprietary "World Engine" runs skill checks against player stats, tracks hit points numerically, and processes permadeath as an actual game state. Our [full Voyage review](/blog/voyage-review) rates it 4/5.

New to the category? Our [AI Dungeon Master guide](/blog/ai-dungeon-master) explains how AI DMs work and where each style fits.

## Mechanics and Consequences

This is the entire reason Voyage exists. AI Dungeon has essentially no mechanics by design — there's nothing to learn and nothing to push back against, which is exactly what some players want and exactly what leaves others feeling like their choices don't carry weight. Succeeding at something feels identical to the AI simply agreeing with you.

Voyage's World Engine changes that completely: real skill checks, numerically tracked hit points, spatial positioning, and — the headline feature — permanent character death. When your character dies in Voyage, they're gone for good; a new character begins in the same world, inheriting consequences from the prior run. Risk aversion becomes rational. Preparation starts mattering. It's the same technology lineage, rebuilt around the principle that a story you can lose is worth playing more than a story you can't lose.

## Memory and Consistency

Neither platform is a memory powerhouse, but Voyage's structured approach holds up better over a session. AI Dungeon treats every session as one continuous text-generation task, which means it hallucinates past events, occasionally resurrects characters who died, and loses spatial and plot logic as the context window fills — premium tiers expand that window, but the underlying architecture still struggles over dozens of sessions.

Voyage's World Engine tracks state numerically rather than relying purely on generative memory — hit points, positioning, and death are actual game state, not just narrative claims the model has to remember correctly. That doesn't make Voyage's storytelling memory flawless, but the mechanical backbone gives it a consistency floor that AI Dungeon's pure-narrative approach doesn't have.

## Multiplayer

Voyage supports synchronous co-op for up to four players through a smart mechanic: the host's action pool covers the session, but guests join and play without consuming their own quota — and because permadeath is real, groups share the weight of consequences together. AI Dungeon's multiplayer exists but is casual and asynchronous by nature, fine for collaborative storytelling passed between friends but not built around shared stakes the way Voyage's co-op is.

## Platform and Access

AI Dungeon is fully open today — web, mobile, and a dedicated Steam version with offline flexibility and a one-time-purchase option. Its Unchained update also made the free tier genuinely usable, removing the old energy and ad systems that used to interrupt play.

Voyage is mobile-first (iOS and Android) and currently gated behind an invite-code expanded beta with a real waitlist — Latitude has said an open beta is scheduled for later in 2026, with no exact date announced. It's optimized for short-burst, pick-up-and-play sessions rather than desk-bound desktop play, which is a deliberate design choice, not an oversight — but it does mean Voyage isn't a drop-in replacement for AI Dungeon today for anyone without an invite.

## Price

AI Dungeon's free tier is usable and ad-free since Unchained, with paid tiers running from $14.99/month (Journey) up to $99.99/month (Ultimate) for the largest premium models and biggest context windows — a price ceiling many players find hard to justify for a text experience. Voyage's anticipated pricing runs $15/month (Standard) to $50/month (Ultimate) at launch, which looks more defensible against AI Dungeon's top end, especially given the structured World Engine AI Dungeon lacks entirely. Voyage's free beta tier offers a starter usage pool with daily claims for players who can get access.
