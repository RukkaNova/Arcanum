There's a question worth settling before you subscribe to NovelAI: do you want to *write* a story, or do you want to *play* one? Because NovelAI answers the first question better than almost anything else on the market, and the second one less well than it used to. Get the distinction right and it's a joy. Get it wrong and you'll spend a subscription wondering why the most beautiful prose engine you've ever used keeps tripping over its own plot.

You'll find NovelAI in our [client directory](/clients/novelai), and weighed directly against its old rival in [AI Dungeon vs NovelAI](/blog/ai-dungeon-vs-novelai). This is the full review.

## What NovelAI Is

NovelAI, made by Anlatan, is an AI writing studio. Rather than a chat window or an adventure prompt, it hands you an editor: you write, the model continues, and you steer the result — rewriting, retrying, and shaping the direction as you go. Its models are tuned specifically on fiction, and that single design choice is the root of both what it's brilliant at and where it falls short. It launched in 2021 with a focus on prose and privacy, and it has kept that identity while the AI RPG space has moved on around it.

## The Prose Is Genuinely Different

This is the reason to be here, and it's not a small thing. Most of NovelAI's competition runs on models trained to *answer questions*, and you can feel it — their narration reads like a helpful assistant describing a scene from the outside. NovelAI reads like a novel. The rhythm, the restraint, the willingness to let a moment breathe instead of summarising it — these are the marks of a model trained on books, and once you've felt the difference it's hard to unfeel.

For a writer, that quality compounds. The sentences are better, so the story you're building is better, so the hours you put in feel like they're going somewhere. If prose is what you care about, nothing in this directory matches it.

## The Lorebook: A Worldbuilder Disguised as a Memory Feature

NovelAI's other real strength is the **Lorebook**, and the best way to understand it is not as a memory patch but as a worldbuilding tool. You create entries — characters, factions, places, rules, history — and the model pulls them in when they're relevant, so the flavourful detail of your setting actually stays in play across a long manuscript.

I found this genuinely welcome. So much of the work of a sustained story is keeping track of what you've established, and a tool that helps you *build* the world and *retain* its texture is exactly the kind of scaffolding this medium needs. Paired with the collaborative and novel-writing features, the Lorebook makes NovelAI a legitimately excellent environment for building stories rather than playing games.

## Where It Falls Behind as a Game

Here's the honest other side, and it's why this isn't a higher score. As an AI RPG, NovelAI no longer leads the pack, and the weaknesses are consistent.

- **Reasoning.** Its reasoning is noticeably weaker than the current alternatives. It's a writer, not a thinker — ask it to hold a complex situation together logically and the cracks show in a way they don't on reasoning-strong models.
- **No live knowledge.** It doesn't browse the internet as it answers, so it simply misses knowledge that a connected model would have on hand. For grounded or reference-heavy play, that's a real gap.
- **Plot holes and inconsistency.** In longer play it creates plot holes with some regularity and drifts into inconsistency. The Lorebook helps you *store* lore, but the model's handling of it isn't airtight, and over a long session that adds up.

None of this matters much if you're writing a novel and steering every beat yourself. It matters a lot if you want the AI to *run* a game for you — to reason about consequences, stay consistent, and not contradict what it established an hour ago. That's the line NovelAI sits on the wrong side of in 2026.

The underlying memory limit is the same one every tool in this space fights — worth understanding in [why AI campaigns fall apart](/blog/why-llms-forget) — but NovelAI's reasoning and consistency gaps are felt more sharply because the prose sets such a high expectation.

## Pricing

There's no genuinely usable free tier — only a limited trial — so meaningful use means subscribing, roughly $10 (Tablet) to $25 (Opus) per month, with the top tier unlocking the best model and the largest context. Image generation is bundled in. The flat pricing undercuts the most expensive tiers of some rivals, but the lack of a real free tier raises the barrier to even trying it, which is a notable contrast to something like [AI Dungeon](/blog/ai-dungeon-review), whose free tier is genuinely playable.

## Who It's For

NovelAI is for **writers**: people building long-form stories and worlds who value prose quality and persistent lore above mechanics, reasoning, or current knowledge. If that's you, it's close to ideal and the subscription is easy to justify.

It's the wrong tool if you want a *game* — something that reasons about your choices, enforces consistency, and runs a world that holds together across sessions. For that, a database-backed platform like [RoleForge](/clients/roleforge) or the wider field in [best AI roleplay platforms](/blog/best-ai-roleplay-platforms) will serve you better, and an [Arcanum Original](/arcanum-games) lets you bring a stronger, reasoning-capable frontier model to the table. If you've decided NovelAI isn't it, our [NovelAI alternatives guide](/blog/novelai-alternatives) sorts the options by exactly what you want instead.

## Verdict

NovelAI is the best writing studio in this directory and one of the slipping AI games in it, and both of those things are true at once. Come for the novel-grade prose and the Lorebook worldbuilder; stay if your goal is to author fiction. If your goal is to play a game that thinks, look elsewhere.

**NovelAI Rating: 3/5** — A superb AI writing tool with prose and worldbuilding nothing here matches, held back as an RPG by weak reasoning, no live knowledge, and recurring plot holes. Brilliant for writers; outclassed for players.
