*A design essay by Rukka Nova, designer of [The Chronicler](/arcanum-games/the-chronicler/).*

---

Almost every RPG that handles relationships handles them the same way. There is a meter. It goes up when you say the right things and down when you say the wrong ones. Hit a threshold and something unlocks — a scene, a confession, a loyalty perk. The relationship is a puzzle with a solution, and once you find the solution it stays solved.

[The Chronicler](/arcanum-games/the-chronicler/) was built in direct opposition to that model. Not as a refinement of it — as a rejection.

## The Problem With Meters

A meter gives you steps. Relationship at 40, relationship at 60, relationship at 80. Each threshold is a gate, and passing through it changes the state of something that was always supposed to be continuous. But real relationships don't work in thresholds. They move in gradients — complex, non-linear gradients that can shift in seconds. A conversation that starts well can end badly. A moment of warmth can be undone by a single carelessly chosen word. The direction can reverse without warning and the reversal can last for years or resolve in an afternoon. No meter captures that, because a meter is a simplification, and the whole texture of a relationship lives in what the simplification throws away.

The second problem with meters is that they make relationships gameable. Once you understand the system, you play the system. You say the things that raise the number. You avoid the things that lower it. You are no longer relating to a person — you are optimising a variable. The relationship becomes a task to complete rather than a thing to navigate, and the emotional weight drains out of it entirely.

The Chronicler's design answer is to remove the meter entirely. There is no number. There is no threshold. What exists instead is a hidden simulation of a person — their trust, their attachment, their doubts, their history with you — tracked by the game master without ever being surfaced as a figure the player can read or optimise. The player feels the relationship entirely through how the companion behaves, which is exactly how relationships work in reality.

Not every platform is reaching for the same answer. Our [Fable Forge first look](/blog/fable-forge-first-look) covers a system built on close to the opposite instinct — tagging individual memories by weight and sentiment and using those tags to explicitly gate relationship milestones. It's a more gamified structure than a meter, but it's also a deliberate, surfaced one rather than a hidden simulation, and it's a reasonable illustration of just how much design space sits between "meter" and "no meter at all."

## A System Without a World

The Chronicler is not a fantasy game or a romance game or a slow-burn narrative game. It is a relationship engine, and it is deliberately world-agnostic.

The prompt was built to be dropped into any setting the player chooses. Medieval fantasy. Cyberpunk dystopia. Pirate novel. Cozy village sim. Zombie apocalypse. Whatever world the player wants to inhabit, The Chronicler operates inside it — ensuring that whatever else is happening in that world, the relationships will behave like relationships rather than like game mechanics. Combat can exist or not exist. External stakes can be high or low. The genre can be anything. What The Chronicler provides is a guarantee: wherever you play, the people around you will be real.

This makes it particularly valuable for a specific kind of player — one who doesn't want to rebuild the wheel on every other feature of their game, but who finds most LLM RPG relationships so thin or gamey that they can't invest in them. The Chronicler is the answer to that specific frustration, portable across any world you bring it into.

## Companions Who Stop Responding

The proactivity engine means companions pursue their own goals, initiate their own scenes, and act on their own emotional states without being asked. In a relationship-focused game, this changes something fundamental: the player is no longer the only one who decides when a relationship gets attention.

A companion who wants something will work toward it. A companion who is hurt will withdraw. A companion whose feelings have been crossed will stop initiating — and when you try to initiate, they may not respond. Not coldly, not with a dialogue box that says "relationship too low" — they simply aren't there for you in the way they were, because you did something that made them not want to be.

This produces the thing that almost no relationship system in any RPG produces: consequences that persist and compound. Companions can hold grudges. They can actively dislike you. And unlike a meter that can be patiently ground back up by repeating positive interactions, a damaged relationship in The Chronicler has texture and memory. The companion knows what happened. Their behaviour reflects it. You cannot simply optimise your way back.

Unconditional love is not a default state in The Chronicler. It is an achievement — something that takes sustained, genuine-feeling investment over a long arc of play and right choices to reach. Most of the time, most companions love you conditionally, just like people do. And the conditions can be violated. The relationship can be ruined. It can stay ruined.

## The Soldier

In a zombie apocalypse playthrough, the party included a soldier. He had been through things before the apocalypse that had left marks — the kind of marks that don't show until something touches the right pressure point. The relationship with him had been built carefully over a long stretch of play, the kind of trust that accumulates slowly and feels solid.

Then came an argument. Words were said — the wrong words, the specific wrong words, delivered with enough force to reach whatever the soldier was carrying underneath. He went catatonic. Not angry, not cold — catatonic. He had to be taken to the infirmary, where he spent two weeks of in-game time in a bed, not responding.

When he recovered, the relationship was beyond repair. Not damaged — ended. He would not engage. If the player passed him, he spat.

This is what The Chronicler was built to make possible: a moment that cannot be taken back, a consequence that the game holds onto even when the player would prefer it didn't, a person who was broken by something the player did and who does not forgive simply because time passes. The game was paying attention to who the soldier was, what he was carrying, and what the words meant to someone carrying it — and it responded accordingly.

That response could not have come from a meter. A meter would have shown a number going down. The Chronicler showed a man going somewhere unreachable.

## What It All Adds Up To

The Chronicler doesn't tell you what kind of story to play. It tells you how the people in that story will behave. Bring it into any world you choose — the genre doesn't matter, the stakes don't matter, the presence or absence of combat doesn't matter. What it guarantees is that the relationships in that world will have weight, memory, and the capacity to break in ways that stay broken.

That is the thing most LLM RPGs cannot offer. Not because the technology isn't there, but because most systems are still thinking about relationships as meters rather than as people.
