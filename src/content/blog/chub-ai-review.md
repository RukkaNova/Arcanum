Chub AI (also known as Chub Venus, at chub.ai) is the platform people mention in the same breath as Janitor AI — the *other* big character-card site, with a reputation for permissiveness and an enormous catalogue. After spending real time with it, the split verdict is unusually clean: **the library is one of the best resources in the entire space, and the platform wrapped around it is one of the weakest.** Which of those matters more to you decides whether Chub AI is worth your time.

You can find Chub AI in our [client directory](/clients/chub-ai) for the quick side-by-side view. This review covers what the experience is actually like. *(Chub AI is a third-party platform; Arcanum is independent and unaffiliated, and all names are used nominatively for identification.)*

## What Is Chub AI?

Chub AI is a character-card repository with a chat platform attached — in that order. Users upload, tag, and browse character cards in the open format that has become the standard across AI roleplay, and any card can be chatted with directly on the site. You can run conversations on Chub's own hosted models with zero setup, or connect an external model of your choice through an API key or OpenRouter.

That "in that order" matters. Chub began life as a card hub (the name is literally Character Hub), and it still behaves like one: the catalogue is the product, and the chat experience feels like the feature that got bolted on.

## The Genuine Strengths

**Flexibility is real.** Cards, lorebooks, presets, prompt-level customization — everything is open to you. Where Character.AI locks you into its model and its rules, Chub AI is closer in spirit to [SillyTavern](/blog/sillytavern-guide): the platform assumes you want control and gives it to you.

**Model support is extensive.** Hosted in-house models for instant, no-setup play, plus bring-your-own-model through standard APIs and OpenRouter for anyone who wants a frontier-quality engine behind their character. Covering both ends of that spectrum is genuinely uncommon — [Janitor AI](/blog/janitor-ai) does it too, but few others manage both.

**The card ecosystem is the headline.** The catalogue is enormous and permissive, cards port cleanly to and from other platforms, and the community pushes the format further than most: a large share of the interesting "characters" here are really full scenarios, worlds, or multi-act plots wearing a character card as a container. If you've read our [character card guide](/blog/how-to-write-a-character-card), Chub is where you'll find the format's power users.

## Where It Falls Apart

**There is no structure.** Chub AI hands you a giant pile of cards, a list of models, and a settings page — and that's the whole experience. No onboarding, no guided path from "I found a card" to "I'm having a good session," nothing that shapes play into more than raw chat. Platforms like [Friends & Fables](/clients/friends-and-fables) demonstrate what structure adds; Chub demonstrates what its absence costs.

**It lacks polish, badly.** The interface looks amateurish next to any mainstream competitor — inconsistent layouts, unfinished-feeling flows, design that reads like a side project. That's forgivable in an open-source tool; it's harder to forgive in a platform asking for a subscription.

**Hosted model quality is inconsistent.** Sessions swing between serviceable and poor depending on which model you're on and how it's configured. The floor is low, and a newcomer has no way to know why their first conversation fell flat — or that the fix is buried in model selection and presets.

**It isn't intuitive.** This is the rare hosted platform that's *harder* to get right than the power-user tools it resembles. SillyTavern is honest about being a cockpit; Chub AI looks like a website anyone can use, then quietly expects cockpit-level configuration knowledge to get a good result.

## Chub AI vs Janitor AI

The comparison everyone actually wants: they serve the same use case — big permissive card library, hosted models plus bring-your-own — and Janitor AI simply executes it better. Janitor's experience is more coherent, its community surface is friendlier, and its weak spots (the in-house model, the proxy setup) are well-mapped with [known workarounds](/blog/how-to-use-janitor-ai). Chub's advantages are at the margins: a somewhat more open catalogue and more built-in model routes. As a *destination*, Janitor wins comfortably; as a *library*, Chub holds its own against anyone. The full point-by-point breakdown is in [Chub AI vs Janitor AI](/blog/chub-ai-vs-janitor-ai).

## Who Is Chub AI Actually For?

- **Card hunters.** If you play on SillyTavern or Janitor AI and want source material, Chub's catalogue is a first-rate resource regardless of everything else in this review.
- **Tinkerers** who enjoy configuration for its own sake and don't need polish.
- **Nobody who wants a first platform.** A newcomer to AI roleplay will have a better week starting almost anywhere else — Janitor AI for character chat, or a [structured AI RPG](/clients) if they want an actual game.

## Pricing

Free to browse and chat, with paid subscriptions unlocking the stronger hosted models and perks; bring-your-own-model costs whatever your API provider charges. The content stance is permissive — mature content exists on the platform and is age-gated, which we note factually; the wider landscape of that choice is mapped in our [uncensored AI roleplay guide](/blog/uncensored-ai-roleplay).

## Final Verdict

Chub AI is a great warehouse and a poor storefront. The card library and the flexibility around it are genuinely first-rate — as a *resource*, it has earned its place in the ecosystem, and power users will keep mining it for worlds and plots no matter what any review says. But as a *platform* — the thing you'd actually spend your evening inside — it's unstructured, unpolished, unintuitive, and inconsistent, and it trails every direct competitor at the job it's nominally selling.
