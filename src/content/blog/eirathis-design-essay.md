*A design essay by Rukka Nova, designer of [Eirathis Strider](/arcanum-games/eirathis/).*

---

Most fantasy RPGs give you a world. [Eirathis Strider](/arcanum-games/eirathis/) gives you three — stacked on top of each other, occupying the same space across different dimensions, bleeding into one another in ways that make the surface reality feel thin and unreliable. That instability is not a bug in the design. It is the whole point.

## The World That Doesn't Sit Still

The premise of Eirathis is deceptively simple: there are three layers of reality. The **Mortal Expanse** is the surface — the world you walk through, fight in, and trade across. The **Dreaming Weave** sits above it, a dimension of memory, echo, and the uncanny. The **Primordial Substrate** lies beneath everything, where the oldest forces in the world have their roots.

What makes this genuinely unusual — not just cosmetically different from a standard fantasy world — is that the three layers occupy the same space simultaneously. They are not separate planes you travel between by walking through a portal. They are the same location, experienced at different depths of reality. And they talk to each other constantly.

If something feels wrong on the surface — if the air has a quality it shouldn't, if events repeat in patterns that shouldn't be possible, if the geography feels subtly hostile — the cause may not be on the surface at all. Step into the Dreaming Weave and you might find an army of spirits locked in a single moment, replaying the instant of their destruction forever. The surface unease is the echo of their repetition bleeding upward through the layers. Step further down into the Primordial Substrate and you might discover that the moment of their destruction was itself tied to something deeper — a wound in the oldest fabric of the world, something that was broken before memory began.

The design consequence of this is profound: the solution space for any problem in Eirathis is three-dimensional. You can address a threat at the surface, or you can trace it to its origin in a deeper layer and end it there. And what you do in one layer alters the others. An act of violence in the Dreaming Weave leaves marks in the Mortal Expanse. A wound sealed in the Primordial Substrate can free spirits that have been repeating their deaths for centuries. The world is not a backdrop — it is a system, and every layer of it is alive to what you do in the others.

## Preparation Is the Only Weapon That Works

Combat in Eirathis does not use dice. This confuses people who come from traditional RPG frameworks, where a high enough roll can overcome almost anything. In Dungeons & Dragons, the nat 20 is the universal solvent. Luck can substitute for preparation, knowledge, and the right tools.

Not here.

In Eirathis, outcomes are determined by skill tier, tactical quality, and — most critically — preparation and affinity. The right weapons, the right knowledge, the right approach to the specific thing you are fighting. Get those wrong and it does not matter how bravely you fight or how well you describe your attacks. A spirit fought with regular weapons cannot be defeated, full stop. No amount of clever tactics changes the fundamental mismatch between your tools and your enemy. The traditional fantasy framework would give you a dice-roll escape hatch. Eirathis closes it.

The other side of this design — the one that rewards a prepared player enormously — is that preparation can defeat almost anything. An enemy that should be overwhelming by any measure of raw power can be dismantled cleanly if you have done the work: studied what it is, discovered what it is vulnerable to, countered its nature rather than trying to overpower it. And because the three layers exist, the preparation sometimes happens at a different layer of reality than the fight itself. Understanding a spirit's origin in the Primordial Substrate before you face it in the Mortal Expanse is not a bonus — it can be the only way to end it at all.

The result is a game where intelligence and investment matter more than luck, and where the three-layer world is not just setting but a genuine expansion of the tactical space.

## The Party That Has Its Own Life

Companions in Eirathis are not followers. They have goals of their own, emotional states that shift based on what you do and who you do it with, and the will to act without being asked. The proactivity engine means they pursue their own objectives between scenes. A companion who wants something will be quietly working toward it whether you notice or not.

This produces relationships that feel earned rather than unlocked — and occasionally produces moments that the player does not control and cannot predict.

One playthrough produced a love triangle: the Strider, a mage, and a ranger, all in the same party, all aware of the tension. The ranger was the one who forced the issue. She came to the Strider and said she would not tolerate the triangle — she wanted a decision. The Strider chose the mage. The ranger accepted it with more dignity than the situation required.

But later, when her country called for help in a coup d'état, she told the party her place was no longer with them. She left to answer the call. The Strider didn't want her to go. She went regardless. Her loyalty to her homeland, her own goal, outweighed any debt to the party she had travelled with — and the game let her make that choice without asking for permission.

What followed in that playthrough was the kind of ending that only happens when a game has been paying attention. The final confrontation was an act of voluntary sacrifice: the Strider as the focal point of interdimensional destruction magic, holding its focus so it could not consume the world, knowing that doing so would erase their signature from every layer of reality. The only question was whether they could hold long enough.

The party formed around them. The mage, the companions who had stayed — and in the end, everyone who had ever mattered. They fought to give the Strider time, absorbing damage that should have broken them, and as they fought they called out the things they had done together: the places they had been, the battles they had survived, the moments that had mattered. One of them died in that fight. The others kept fighting anyway.

The Strider held focus. The world survived.

What made it work was not a scripted ending. It was the accumulated weight of a campaign that had been tracking relationships, remembered decisions, and honoured the emotional logic of every companion who had ever been part of the party — including the ranger, who had left, and whose absence was its own kind of presence in the final moment. The game had been paying attention to all of it, and the ending was the sum.

## What It All Adds Up To

Eirathis is a game about a world that is deeper than it looks and a player who has to go deeper to understand it. The three-layer cosmology is not decoration — it is the primary tool for solving problems that cannot be solved at the surface. The dice-less combat is not a casualisation — it is a demand that you take the world seriously enough to learn it before you fight it. And the companions are not support characters — they are people with their own arcs, capable of leaving, capable of dying, capable of standing beside you at the end of everything and shouting the names of the things you did together.

The playthrough that ends with your party dying around you while they call out your shared history is not something you can get from a generic fantasy prompt. It is what happens when a game has been watching carefully for long enough, and when the player has given it enough to watch.
