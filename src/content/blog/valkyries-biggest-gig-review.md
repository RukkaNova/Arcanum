[Valkyrie's Biggest Gig](/games/valkyries-biggest-gig) is a copy-paste prompt by FistFulofValkyries that turns Claude into a gamemaster for a gritty solo cyberpunk RPG. On paper that's a crowded corner of the hobby — cyberpunk is the default aesthetic for half the AI RPGs out there. What makes this one worth a separate look isn't the neon or the megacorps; it's a single design choice in the prompt that quietly reshapes how you play: it makes your *body* the resource you spend.

Most RPGs treat the character as a fixed pole around which the story turns. Valkyrie's Biggest Gig treats the character as something you progressively trade away and rebuild. That's the lens for this review — what the augmentation focus does well, and where the prompt's other instincts undercut it.

## What Valkyrie's Biggest Gig Is

You paste the prompt and Claude opens with a brief character-creation Q&A: your role, backstory, motivations, and starting traits. From there it paints the opening dystopia, hands you a first job, and resolves your actions through combat, hacking, and social intrigue. The instructions explicitly call for morally grey scenarios, escalating nemeses, permadeath, and rewards paid out in credits and cybernetic upgrades. It runs on Claude and slots in alongside the other options in the [best Claude RPGs](/blog/best-claude-rpgs) roundup.

It runs smoothly and it scales. This is a prompt built for a long campaign, not a one-shot, and it holds its shape well enough to support one.

## The Augmentation Loop Is the Hook

The standout is how heavily the prompt weights cybernetic modification. Limb loss and augmentation come up constantly — not as occasional set-pieces but as a recurring pressure — because the instructions keep the body in play. The effect is that you stop protecting your character and start *editing* them. A bad fight costs you an arm; the replacement is better than the original but changes who you are; the next job is shaped by what you've become. Over a campaign that produces genuinely novel transhuman characters who have modified themselves past recognition to survive, and that arc is more interesting than the loot-and-level progression most RPG prompts default to.

The prompt also names the right counterweight: cyberpsychosis. Augmentation is supposed to carry a mental cost as well as a physical one, so that chroming yourself up isn't a free power-up but a slow erosion of who you are. When it surfaces, it's the most interesting tension in the game — the chrome that keeps you alive is also taking your mind. It doesn't surface as often as it should, partly because the reward economy (more on that below) keeps handing you upgrades faster than the consequences can land, but the framework is there, and a player who leans into it gets a much darker, more honest transhuman arc.

The escalation system supports the long game too. Defeat a nemesis and the prompt introduces a bigger one, which keeps the stakes climbing across a campaign. It's a smart way to fight the natural deflation of a story that's already had its big villain — though the hand-off can occasionally feel forced, a new antagonist arriving because the structure demands one rather than because the story earned it.

## Where the Reward Economy Breaks It

The biggest problem is generosity. The prompt instructs the gamemaster to award credits, upgrades, and intel for big scores, and Claude takes that brief enthusiastically — money and cybernetics get thrown at you left and right, and the game drifts into being too easy. In a genre that's supposed to be about scarcity and desperate trade-offs, abundance is the wrong failure mode. It also undercuts the augmentation loop above: cyber-modification is only meaningful as a *sacrifice*, and when upgrades are free, the body stops feeling like a resource you're spending.

The good news is that this is a prompt, so it's the easiest thing in the world to fix — trim or rewrite the reward guidance and the difficulty curve falls back into place. It's worth doing. This is the one change I'd make before starting a serious campaign, and it's a useful reminder that paste-in prompts are editable in a way packaged games like the [Arcanum Originals](/arcanum-games) aren't.

## The Cliché Problem

The world itself is cyberpunk by the numbers: corporate exploitation, urban decay, credits over human rights. None of that is wrong, but none of it is surprising either — it's the genre's greatest hits, competently delivered. The prose also tilts toward describing the cityscape over the people in it, so the dystopia can feel more painted than populated. You get atmosphere; you get fewer of the distinct, motivated characters that make a world feel lived-in.

This is a recurring theme across prompt-based RPGs that carry no lore of their own — the same generalist trade I covered in the [Solo RPG Master review](/blog/solo-rpg-master-review) and the [Burning Sun V2 review](/blog/burning-sun-v2-review). Valkyrie's Biggest Gig commits harder to a single genre than those two, which gives it a sharper identity, but it still leans on the player and the model to supply the specificity the prompt doesn't.

## Who It's For

Play Valkyrie's Biggest Gig if you want a smooth, scalable cyberpunk campaign on Claude and you're drawn to the transhuman angle — the slow trade of flesh for chrome is the best thing here. Bring a willingness to tune the reward economy down, and you'll get a much grittier, more honest version of the game the prompt is reaching for.

Look elsewhere if you want a deeply original setting out of the box, or if you don't enjoy editing prompts to taste. For the wider field, the [public games directory](/games) compares it against world-first and character-first alternatives.

## Verdict

Valkyrie's Biggest Gig is a capable, customizable cyberpunk RPG with one genuinely good idea — making the body something you spend — wrapped in a world that plays the genre straight. It runs smoothly, scales to long campaigns, and produces memorable transhuman characters. It's held back by a reward economy so generous it removes the tension, and by a cliché setting described more vividly than it's populated. Fix the economy yourself and it's a notably better game than it ships as.

**Valkyrie's Biggest Gig Rating: 3/5** — A smooth, scalable Claude cyberpunk RPG with a standout cybernetic-augmentation loop, capped by a too-easy economy and a by-the-numbers world.
