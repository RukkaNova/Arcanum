The Chronicler is a persistent, relationship-driven text RPG built for Claude Projects. You play the Wayfinder — every word and action is yours alone — while a cast of companions pursues their own goals, builds or breaks trust with you on their own terms, and takes independent action in the world when the momentum stalls. There are no affection meters, no romance unlocks. The RomanceCronos engine, loaded as a knowledge file, governs attraction, trust, intimacy, and withdrawal through behavior and consistency alone.

Two files do all the work: the master prompt sets the game's laws and hidden tracking systems, and RomanceCronos is the dedicated relationship rulebook the engine consults for every interpersonal beat. Load both into a Claude Project and the system runs across every conversation in it — long campaigns, persistent companions, and a world that keeps moving whether or not you do.

## What Makes The Chronicler Different

Most AI roleplay treats relationships like a vending machine. Say the right things often enough and the meter fills. The Chronicler was built on a different premise entirely: if a relationship can be measured, it is already broken.

The RomanceCronos engine does not use numeric affection values, hidden relationship meters, or unlock thresholds. It has no concept of "romance triggered at 75 points." Instead, it tracks the things that actually shape how people feel about each other — consistency, discretion, follow-through, and whether your actions match your words over time. A companion who trusts you trusts you because you earned it, turn by turn, in the same way trust works between real people. One who withdraws does so because something happened that warranted withdrawal. The system never announces these shifts. It expresses them through behavior.

This has one important consequence: you cannot game it. There is no dialogue option that shortcuts intimacy. There is no pattern of inputs that reliably produces a specific output. What the engine produces is a cast of characters who respond to who you actually are in the game, not to who you perform yourself to be.

## The Proactivity Engine

Companions in The Chronicler do not wait. Every in-world day, each companion evaluates their personal goal, their current emotional state, and the tensions they are carrying — then takes one small action toward what they want, independent of you. One sharpens her blade while stealing glances in your direction. Another avoids eye contact all evening and drinks alone. A third quietly asks around about something they have been searching for since before you met them.

This engine is what gives the game its texture. You are not the only thing happening in the world. People have their own lives, their own trajectories, and their own opinions about where things are going — and those opinions change based on what they observe, not just what you tell them directly.

When the momentum slows, companions initiate scenes on their own. When a relationship hits a threshold, a companion acts on it. When jealousy develops — because you pursued someone else, or because of something they saw — they respond in the way that fits who they are. None of this requires your input. It is the world continuing to move.

## Desire, Trust, and the Distinction That Matters

A companion can want you without trusting you. They can trust you completely without desire ever entering the picture. They can feel both, or neither, and the engine tracks these as distinct states that evolve independently. This separation is the design principle that sets The Chronicler apart from most relationship systems, which collapse desire and trust into a single "affection" number and call it done.

The practical consequence is that intimacy — when it happens — means something. It is not unlocked by reaching a threshold. It is the result of a specific combination of accumulated experience, mutual understanding, and the right moment. It can be undone by carelessness or betrayal, and it does not automatically regenerate when the offense is forgotten. Trust, once broken, recovers slowly or not at all depending on what broke it and what happened afterward.

Fade-to-black applies throughout — the engine describes the approach to intimacy and its emotional aftermath, never the explicit act itself.

## Absolute Player Agency

The Chronicler enforces complete player sovereignty as a non-negotiable law. The game master never narrates your speech, actions, thoughts, or feelings. It describes the world and asks what you do. It will not fast-forward time, skip a conversation, or end a scene without your consent — narration halts at every threshold and waits. If anything oversteps, an Agency Check rewinds to your last decision point.

A Character Insight command gives you one evocative sentence on where a companion currently stands — never numbers, never the hidden logs, just the emotional truth of the moment.

## Who This Game Is For

The Chronicler is for players who care more about people than mechanics — who want an AI campaign where a companion's genuine trust is the reward worth working for, and a betrayal is the cost worth feeling. It is built for patience, for the long session, for the player who would rather sit in a difficult conversation than skip past it.

If you have ever wanted an AI RPG that treats its characters as people rather than resources, this is the one built for you.
